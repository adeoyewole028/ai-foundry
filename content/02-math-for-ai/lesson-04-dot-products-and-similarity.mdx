## Plain-English Explanation

A dot product compares two vectors by multiplying matching positions and adding the results.

In plain English: it gives a score that can tell whether two vectors point in a similar direction.

## Key Terms

### Dot product

Simple meaning:
A similarity score made from two vectors.

Real-world analogy:
Comparing two shopping lists by checking which categories line up.

Technical meaning:
The sum of pairwise products from two equal-length vectors.

Example:
`[1, 2] dot [3, 4]` becomes `(1 * 3) + (2 * 4) = 11`.

Why it matters:
Search, recommendations, and embedding comparisons often depend on similarity scoring.

## Mental Model

Two arrows pointing in the same direction produce a stronger score. Arrows pointing in different directions produce a weaker score.

## Example

A document search system can compare a question vector with document vectors and rank the closest matches.

## Code Walkthrough

```python
query = [0.9, 0.1, 0.3]
document = [0.8, 0.2, 0.4]

score = sum(a * b for a, b in zip(query, document))
print(score)
```

Higher scores usually mean stronger alignment, assuming the vectors are prepared correctly.

## Common Mistakes

- Comparing vectors with different meanings.
- Forgetting that length and scale can affect raw scores.
- Treating the highest score as guaranteed truth instead of best available match.
